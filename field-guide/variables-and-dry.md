# Variables and DRY

*Lesson 1*

**Keywords:** `variable` · `DRY` · `Don't Repeat Yourself` · `hardcoded` · `refactor`

---

**YOU'LL HEAR THIS WHEN** *you're in a planning or engineering meeting*

A developer says: *"That value is hardcoded in a bunch of places"* or *"We need to refactor that."*

You might be in a sprint planning meeting as a PM or ops lead when a change that sounds simple turns out to be complicated. Now you know why: DRY has been violated.

---

**YOU MIGHT SAY THIS WHEN** *you're in an interview about process or operations*

*"I'm drawn to the DRY principle — the idea that if you're doing the same thing in multiple places, you're creating future maintenance work. I try to apply that to processes, not just code."*

This lands well in interviews for product, operations, or strategy roles at tech companies. It signals systems thinking.

---

**WHAT NON-TECHNICAL PEOPLE GET WRONG** *common misconception to quietly hold correctly*

That 'just changing it in one place' is always simple. It's only simple if the code was written with variables and DRY in mind. If it wasn't, that 'one change' means hunting through an entire codebase. This is why engineers push back on requests that sound easy.

---

**SO WHAT?** *the one-line reason this matters to you*

When someone says a codebase is 'messy' or 'hard to change,' they often mean DRY has been violated. Understanding this helps you ask better questions about technical debt and timelines.
